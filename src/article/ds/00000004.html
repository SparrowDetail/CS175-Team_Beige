The Nintendo DS Family: A Complete Look at All Variants and Their Hardware

Robert Pope II 10/28/2024

Introduction

The Nintendo DS family, introduced by Nintendo in 2004, redefined the landscape of handheld gaming. Known for its unique dual-screen design, touch-sensitive interface, and innovative software, the DS and its successors built on Nintendo’s legacy of creating accessible, enjoyable, and family-friendly gaming experiences. Over its lifecycle, the DS family sold over 150 million units worldwide, making it one of the best-selling video game systems in history. Let us dive into the different iterations of the Nintendo DS family and explore the impact this handheld system had on the gaming industry.

Nintendo DS

The original Nintendo DS, released in 2004, marked a significant leap in portable gaming. Its key innovation was the dual-screen setup, with the bottom screen being a touch-sensitive display, allowing for a range of new gameplay mechanics. This touch screen could be operated with a stylus or finger, enabling intuitive control schemes that were previously unimaginable on handheld consoles.
The DS also featured built-in Wi-Fi connectivity, which facilitated wireless multiplayer gameplay and, eventually, online services like the Nintendo Wi-Fi Connection. This helped the DS host some of the most popular multiplayer games of its time, such as Mario Kart DS and Pokémon Diamond and Pearl. Two ARM processors (ARM9 and ARM7) running at 67 MHz and 33 MHz, respectively, allowed the DS to handle more complex games than its predecessors Additionally, the DS was backward compatible with Game Boy Advance games, giving it access to a massive library of titles right from its launch.


Nintendo DS Lite

In 2006, Nintendo refined the original DS with the release of the Nintendo DS Lite. This model maintained the functionality of the original but improved on its design, making it sleeker, lighter, and more portable. The device still included the GBA slot for backward compatibility and retained the dual-screen and touch functionality. The DS Lite featured brighter screens, better battery life, and an overall more polished aesthetic, which attracted a broader audience.
The DS Lite became a cultural phenomenon, partly because of its expensive library of casual and educational games. Titles like Brain Age and Nintendogs appealed to non-traditional gamers, helping the DS Lite reach new demographics, including older adults and young children.

Nintendo DSi

The Nintendo DSi, introduced in 2008, brought additional features and hardware improvements to the DS family. The DSi had a slightly larger screen than the DS Lite and featured two cameras—one on the exterior and one facing the player—which allowed for more interactive games and the ability to take photos and record videos.
The DSi also introduced the DSi Ware service, a digital distribution platform where players could download smaller, often experimental games and applications. This addition reflected the broader shift in the industry toward digital content and downloadable games, a trend that would continue to grow in importance in subsequent Nintendo systems. However, one of the major changes in the DSi was the removal of the Game Boy Advance cartridge slot, meaning that this model lost the backward compatibility with GBA titles. In exchange, the DSi offered internal storage and an SD card slot for storing downloadable content, media, and photos.

Nintendo DSi XL

The Nintendo DSi XL, released in 2009, was designed to appeal to users looking for a larger, more comfortable gaming experience. It featured larger screens and a wider body than the original DSi, which made it ideal for sharing games and playing for extended periods. The DSi XL also came pre-loaded with a few DSi Ware titles, enhancing the out-of-box experience.
The DSi XL was particularly popular with older gamers who appreciated the larger screen size and easier readability, further expanding the DS family’s appeal beyond traditional gaming audiences. The DSi XL maintained all the DSi’s features, including its cameras, online store, and SD card support, but with a focus on comfort and visibility.
