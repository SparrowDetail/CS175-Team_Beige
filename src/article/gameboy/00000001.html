Motherboard & Sound System Analysis

 The following picture is the motherboard inside of the Gameboy, with it not containing much and have 8 KB of VRAM for picture. Compared to maybe something like the Super N intendo with 64KB of VRAM as it also has color, it does not seem much in comparison. The Gameboy picture itself would be on a 2.5 inch screen, which was LCD (liquid-crystal display), and the dimensions of 1.9 inches wide and 1.7 inches high. The screen produced a whopping 160 pixels wide by another whopping 144 pixels high for the standard resolution. The ratio for the resolution is 10:9 and has a refresh rate of just about 60Hz. Overall, the hand held device was bigger compared to its screen, with the height being 5.8 inches, width being 3.5 inches and depth being at 1.2. With the harder to see pixels, no color and needing to be around direct lighting or you wouldnt be able to see it due to the type of screen, those were the few main features the newer generation were to fix!

 Audio was processed through the 'Audio Processing Unit' which proceeded to have programmable channels: first being for voulme, how high or how low with the frequency, the second following strictly volume changes for high to low, then to another channel that plays any waveform, a shape on a graph of the shape of a wave, within RAM and lastly white noise that contains volume changes high to low. The Gameboy was very different in its regards for sound, as it was only of the only retro systems to use wave channels within its sound chip. Better names for these channels can even be Duty1, Duty2, Wave and Noise (very simple in regards for what each layer does respectfully).

CPU Overhaul!!

 The Gameboy itself contains what is called a custom SoC(system on a chip). Diving deeper into the CPU, the processor that does most of the work is a Sharp SM83. which contains the Intel 8080 and Zilog Z80 8-bit processors. On startup, the SoC uses a "256 B 'bootstrap' ROM" which is a process that sends through the processor immediately. The 8-bit DMG CPU also contained an 8 bit data bus with 16-bit address in place, reading off of an outstanding 64 KB of memory. The Gameboy is also holding six 16-bit registers (which is just a location that is set to be quickly available,but relies on the processor). One example would reflect towards the picture above, where 'AF' are two good examples of 8-bit registers. 'A' for where operations involing math happens and 'F'for when it can flag other registers for their specific purposes. This was done due to the design of the hybrid CPU, and allowed for best optimization operation and looks wise. Even though old, still has plenty of interesting facts!
